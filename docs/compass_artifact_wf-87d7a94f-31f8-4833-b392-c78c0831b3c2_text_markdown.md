# Reservoir computing for chaotic Rulkov prediction, a decision-ready map

**The shortest answer: the single published paper that most directly solves your problem is Tanaka, Matsumori, Yoshida & Aihara, "Reservoir computing with diverse timescales for prediction of multiscale dynamics," *Phys. Rev. Research* 4, L032014 (2022). They introduce a leaky-integrator ESN in which each reservoir neuron has its own leak rate α_i drawn from a broad (log-uniform) distribution (DTS-ESN), benchmark it on fast-slow chaotic systems including the Rulkov 2-D map, and show it decisively outperforms single-α ESNs on exactly the failure mode you are hitting.** The second-most important result is Zhang & Cornelius, *Phys. Rev. Research* 5, 033213 (2023) ("Catch-22s of reservoir computing"), which quantitatively proves that vanilla NG-RC with polynomial features *diverges* on rational nonlinearities of the β/(1+u²) form unless the exact feature is handed to it, ruling out plain NG-RC as your first move. Everything else (deep ESNs, hybrid knowledge-based RC, Bayesian ESNs, coupled-network parallel schemes) is secondary scaffolding around the DTS-ESN core. Below is the full evidence map, training recipe, and a prioritized trial order.

## Why your vanilla ESN fails, and which three objects you should actually vary

A single-reservoir leaky-integrator ESN (Jaeger, Lukoševičius, Popovici, Siewert 2007, *Neural Networks* 20:335) has one global leak α and one global spectral radius ρ. The Rulkov map with β=4.1, ν=σ=0.001 imposes **two timescales separated by three orders of magnitude**: spike rate O(1) per iteration versus burst envelope O(10²–10³) iterations. A scalar α can preserve either the spike fine structure (α near 1) or the burst envelope (α small), not both. In closed-loop rollout, the error on the unresolved timescale drives a positive-Lyapunov drift off the attractor within a handful of iterations, which is what you are observing. Three levers fix this: **(i) distribute the effective timescales across the reservoir (DTS-ESN or DeepESN), (ii) stabilise closed-loop rollout via training-time noise / Lyapunov-aware regularisation, and (iii) replace MSE with an attractor-matching loss so short-term divergence is not punished more than attractor-climate error.** Each of these is now supported by peer-reviewed results on chaotic benchmarks.

## Heterogeneous-timescale reservoirs are the right class of architecture

**Tanaka et al. 2022 (PRR) is the load-bearing reference.** They tested the Rulkov 2-D map alongside the Hindmarsh–Rose ODE, two-coupled van der Pol, and two-coupled Lorenz. With a 200-unit reservoir whose leak rates α_i are sampled on a log-uniform distribution, the DTS-ESN reached an order-of-magnitude larger "valid prediction time" than a tuned standard LI-ESN at every spectral radius they scanned. The trained readout automatically selects neurons whose τ_i matches the timescale of each output component — essentially, an unsupervised fast/slow decomposition. The reservoir size, random W_in and W sparsity, and ridge β are all standard; **the only change from a conventional ESN is one line of code that samples α_i ~ LogUniform(α_min, α_max)**, which makes DTS-ESN a near-zero-cost upgrade. Caveat: Tanaka reports normalised-valid-time, not Lyapunov-time horizons, and does not report return maps, ISI distributions, or Lyapunov-exponent matching for Rulkov — exactly the attractor statistics you care about. Filling that gap is an open niche.

A close cousin is **DeepESN** (Gallicchio, Micheli & Pedrelli, *Neurocomputing* 268:87, 2017; *Neural Networks* 108:33, 2018; Tortorella, Gallicchio, Micheli, ICANN 2022 "Hierarchical Dynamics in Deep ESNs"). A stack of L reservoirs, layer ℓ receiving only layer ℓ−1, spontaneously develops an **intrinsic hierarchy of timescales** — higher layers drift slower even with identical per-layer parameters. Assigning decreasing contractivity (larger α at bottom, smaller α at top) is proven optimal for multi-timescale regression. Manneschi, Ellis, Gigante, Lin, Del Giudice, Vasilaki, *Front. Appl. Math. Stat.* 6:616658 (2021) "Exploiting multiple timescales in hierarchical ESNs" provides the quantitative multiscale analysis. **DeepESN is architecturally transparent — fast bottom / slow top — but DTS-ESN empirically outperforms it on the only Rulkov benchmark in the literature.**

Two further architectures are worth knowing but are subordinate to DTS-ESN for your target. Pathak, Hunt, Girvan, Lu, Ott, *PRL* 120:024102 (2018) introduced **parallel localized reservoirs** that predict Kuramoto-Sivashinsky for ~8 Lyapunov times by partitioning space — this is about *spatial* decomposition, not temporal, and is only relevant for you when you move to coupled networks. Pathak, Wikner, Fussell, Chandra, Hunt, Girvan, Ott, *Chaos* 28:041101 (2018) "Hybrid forecasting of chaotic processes" is the **hybrid knowledge-based/ML ESN**: feed an imperfect physics model K[u(t)] alongside the reservoir state into the readout. On Lorenz-63 it extends valid time from ≈5 to ≈10 Lyapunov times even with K carrying 5% parameter error. For you this is directly actionable: inject a smoothed or parameter-mismatched Rulkov (e.g. β=4 instead of 4.1, or a piecewise-polynomial approximant of β/(1+u²)) as the knowledge model, and let the reservoir learn the residual. This usually gives the longest valid prediction times of any published RC variant; the price is giving up full black-boxness.

## NG-RC is tempting but does not pass the Rulkov nonlinearity test

Gauthier, Bollt, Griffith & Barbosa, *Nat. Commun.* 12:5564 (2021), proved that an RC with linear nodes and polynomial readout is equivalent to a nonlinear vector autoregression (NVAR) of delay-embedded features; Bollt, *Chaos* 31:013108 (2021) gave the formal equivalence. On Lorenz-63, 400 training points and a polynomial library (k=2, s=1, p=2, 28 features) reach ~5 Lyapunov times of closed-loop validity with millisecond training. On the double-scroll (sinh nonlinearity) cubic features (p=3) reproduce the attractor climate. Kent, Barbosa & Gauthier, *Chaos* 34:023102 (2024) extended NG-RC to the **Hénon map** with only 10 training points and used it for closed-loop chaos control.

**The Rulkov nonlinearity is precisely the class that breaks NG-RC.** Zhang & Cornelius, *Phys. Rev. Research* 5:033213 (2023) study a magnetic-pendulum model with a rational term structurally analogous to β/(1+u²) and show that polynomial NG-RC trajectories *blow up to infinity*; only if the rational feature is supplied exactly does NG-RC succeed, and even a small perturbation of the exponent collapses accuracy to chance — the eponymous Catch-22. A companion paper, Zhang, Roque dos Santos, Zhang & Cornelius, *Chaos* 35:073102 (2025) "How more data can hurt," shows NG-RC trained on more noise-free data becomes *more* unstable because delay-state auxiliary dimensions produce an ill-conditioned integrator; mitigations are SVD solvers and ridge scaling with N_train or noise injection. Roque dos Santos & Bollt (2024/25) on NG-RC numerical instabilities provide the conditioning analysis.

The **multi-scale random-Fourier-feature RC** preprint (arXiv:2511.14775, Nov 2025) is the one explicit attempt to salvage NG-RC-style methods for neuronal bursting: they replace polynomial outer products with multi-scale RFF kernels and benchmark on Rulkov, Izhikevich, Hindmarsh-Rose and Morris-Lecar, showing single-scale (polynomial-style) features underperform. Treat this as preprint evidence that your instinct against plain NG-RC is correct. Haluszczynski, Räth & Patel, *Sci. Rep.* 13 (2023), propose a minimal-RC hybrid between NG-RC and traditional RC that outperforms both NG-RC and SINDy on Lorenz in the low-data regime and could, in principle, handle non-polynomial targets more robustly — it is a reasonable alternative library to try, but has not been benchmarked on Rulkov. **Net recommendation: do not start with NG-RC. If you want to include it eventually, build a hybrid that uses traditional DTS-ESN reservoir features plus a small polynomial block rather than polynomial features alone.**

## Liquid State Machines and spiking reservoirs are a dead end for your use case

Published LSM / spiking-reservoir results on chaotic *prediction* of bursting neurons are essentially absent; LSMs are almost entirely classification-oriented (Maass, Natschläger, Markram, *Neural Computation* 14:2531, 2002). Schrauwen, Büsing & Legenstein (NIPS 2008) analyse connectivity/dynamics trade-offs in binary vs analog reservoirs but report reduced computational performance in dense binary circuits — unfavourable for the high-fidelity closed-loop rollout you need. More recent bio-inspired work (Panahi, Zhai, Haile & Lai, "Neuromorphic reservoir computing," *Chaos* 2025 / preprint) builds physical RC on a **map-based behavioural neural model** — essentially a Rulkov-like simplified neuron used as the reservoir node, not as the prediction target — and succeeds on Lorenz, reproducing largest Lyapunov exponent and KL divergence. This is structurally interesting because it shows Rulkov-type neurons make *good reservoirs*, but it does not address the inverse problem of *predicting* Rulkov. Skip LSMs.

## Bayesian and ensemble RC give you calibrated rollouts but not longer horizons

Chatzis & Demiris, *IEEE TNN* 22:1435 (2011), "Echo State Gaussian Processes," replace the linear readout with a GP over reservoir features and give calibrated posterior predictive distributions on autoregressive rollouts; they outperform ESNs on Mackey-Glass and NARMA-10 in both accuracy and uncertainty calibration. McDermott & Wikle, *Stat* 6:315 (2017), and follow-up *Environmetrics* 2019 build a Bayesian ensemble ESN with shrinkage priors on readout weights, used on spatiotemporal climate nowcasting. For chaotic rollouts specifically, Pathak et al. 2018 implicitly use random-seed ensembles, and Platt, Penny, Smith, Chen & Abarbanel, *Neural Networks* 153:530 (2022) ("A systematic exploration of reservoir computing") use 10–30 multi-seed ensembles and report VPT distributions. Ahmed, Tennie & Magri, *Proc. R. Soc. A* 481:20250550 (2025), produce calibrated confidence bands on extreme-event statistics via quantum-RC ensembles. **Practical takeaway: ensembling 10–30 DTS-ESN seeds and reporting the spread is cheap and turns raw valid-time into calibrated uncertainty; ESGP is worth trying if you want a principled Bayesian alternative, but it will not extend the Lyapunov-time horizon beyond DTS-ESN.**

## The training tricks that actually matter for closed-loop chaos

The training literature has advanced sharply since 2020 and now offers a compact prescription. Five items belong in your pipeline.

First, **generalized teacher forcing**. Hess, Monfared, Brenner & Durstewitz, ICML 2023 (arXiv:2306.04406) prove that linearly interpolating between model state and data-inferred state at each step with mixing α* tuned to the system's maximal Lyapunov exponent gives **strictly bounded Jacobian-product norms** on chaotic targets. On Lorenz-63, Rössler, Lorenz-96 and real mouse neural data, GTF with a shallow PLRNN beats LEM, coRNN, dendPLRNN, LSTM and neural ODEs on state-space divergence and power-spectrum Hellinger distance. The antecedent result, Mikhaeil, Monfared & Durstewitz, NeurIPS 2022 (arXiv:2110.07238), proves BPTT gradients diverge whenever the trained system is chaotic and proposes **sparse teacher forcing** with period τ≈1/λ_max — i.e. one Lyapunov time between data injections. For an ESN trained by ridge regression you cannot literally apply BPTT tricks; the analog is **to train the readout by including occasional closed-loop rollout residuals** rather than only one-step-ahead residuals, which is what "closed-loop training" or Platt-style predictive-generalized-synchronization training does.

Second, **noise injection / LMNT regularization**. Wikner, Harvey, Girvan, Hunt, Pomerance, Antonsen & Ott, *Neural Networks* 170:94 (2024) (arXiv:2211.05262), "Stabilizing Machine Learning Prediction of Dynamics: Noise and Noise-Inspired Regularization," show that either adding small input/state noise during ridge-regression training or applying the deterministic **Linearized Multi-Noise Training (LMNT)** regularizer yields indefinitely stable closed-loop rollouts of Lorenz-63 and KS, where unregularized ESNs diverge. LMNT is, essentially, one extra Jacobian-weighted penalty term in the ridge problem — mechanically cheap. This is the single biggest stability improvement available to a ridge-trained ESN.

Third, **Jacobian-spectrum and Lyapunov-aware validation**. Margazoglou & Magri, *Nonlinear Dynamics* 111:8799 (2023) (arXiv:2210.06167) derive the ESN Jacobian in closed form, extract Lyapunov spectra, covariant Lyapunov vectors and hyperbolicity angles, and introduce the **Recycle Validation** protocol for hyperparameter selection that preserves tangent-space structure. Racca & Magri, *Neural Networks* 142:252 (2021), deploy Bayesian optimization over ESN hyperparameters with chaotic-aware validation windows tied to Lyapunov time, outperforming grid search. **Use Recycle Validation (or Bayesian optimization with BO-GP over log α_min, log α_max, ρ, β, σ_noise) rather than grid search or NRMSE-based tuning.**

Fourth, **attractor-level losses**. Platt, Penny, Smith, Chen & Abarbanel, *Chaos* 33:103107 (2023) "Constraining chaos: Enforcing dynamical invariants in the training of reservoir computers" (arXiv:2304.12865) add Lyapunov-exponent and Kaplan-Yorke-dimension penalties to the RC loss; on Lorenz-96 (F=8) and spectral quasi-geostrophy this markedly extends valid forecast time and attractor fidelity at limited data. Even supplying only the leading LE helps substantially. Jiang, Lu, Orlova & Willett, NeurIPS 2023 (arXiv:2306.01187) "Training neural operators to preserve invariant measures of chaotic attractors" augment MSE with **Sinkhorn/Wasserstein losses on invariant-measure distributions of chosen statistics** (or contrastive-learned statistics); on KS and Lorenz-96 these reproduce the climate where MSE-trained baselines collapse under noise. Platt, Wong, Clark, Penny & Abarbanel, *Chaos* 31:123118 (2021) "Robust forecasting using predictive generalized synchronization" provides an a priori synchronization test and argues Lyapunov-spectrum reproduction, not VPT, is the right success metric. **For Rulkov, the natural attractor-level targets are the ISI distribution, inter-burst interval distribution, 1-D return map of inter-burst maxima, and the largest Lyapunov exponent — add a Wasserstein distance on the ISI histogram to the loss via a short rollout during training.**

Fifth, **washout/warmup, ridge tuning**. Lukoševičius (*Tricks of the Trade*, LNCS 7700, 2012) is still the standard playbook: washout length ≈ memory time scale of reservoir (tens to hundreds of steps); for a DTS-ESN with α_min ≈ 10⁻³ you need ~3·10³ washout steps to reach synchrony with the slow mode. Platt 2022 (arXiv:2201.08910) demonstrates input bias is critical and VPT saturates once the reservoir has reached the synchronization manifold.

## Direct published results on Rulkov, HR, Izhikevich, Chialvo

The direct Rulkov/bursting-RC publication list is short, and it matters for calibration of expectations. **Tanaka et al. 2022 (PRR)** is the only closed-loop study on the full Rulkov 2-D map; DTS-ESN achieves large-factor valid-time gains but does not report Lyapunov-time horizons or return-map matching. **Roy, Senapati, Poria, Mishra & Hens, *Phys. Rev. E* 105:064205 (2022)** use a standard ESN on scale-free networks of Rulkov neurons to predict burst-synchronization order parameters (not individual attractors); they find low-degree-node training is best on assortative graphs. **Roy, Mandal, Hens, Prasad, Kuznetsov & Dev, *Phys. Rev. Research* 2:033338 (2020)**, predict spiking and bursting regimes in complete graphs of Hindmarsh–Rose neurons and Josephson-junction arrays with a tuned-leak ESN, confirming the central role of α. **Phys. Rev. E 108:064304 (2023)** uses a nonlocally-coupled-pendulum RC to predict HR and reports Pearson correlation, Hausdorff metric on the attractor and **largest-Lyapunov-exponent matching** — still rare. **Mandal, Sinha & Shrimali, *Phys. Rev. E* 110:034204 (2024)** uses a 1-D logistic-map reservoir to predict HR in closed loop. **Nakagawa et al. 2025 (HetAESN, IEEE Access/TNNLS)** extends DTS-ESN with sub-reservoirs of distinct τ and outperforms homogeneous AESN on HR. The **arXiv:2511.14775 (2025) multi-scale RFF RC** benchmarks Rulkov, Izhikevich, HR and Morris-Lecar but is not peer-reviewed. **No RC paper on the Chialvo map was found — this is an open niche.**

For background benchmarks, **Nadiga et al. (2022, Machine Learning with Applications)** compare LSTM, GRU, ESN, hybrid ESN and NVAR on a bursting Morris-Lecar neuron alongside Mackey-Glass and Lorenz-63, with qualitative attractor agreement but no Lyapunov-time numbers. **Zheng et al., *IEEE Access* 8:91188 (2020)** (LS-ESN, the paper you already know) was tested on Lorenz, sunspots and power-load, not on any bursting neuron. **None of the published papers quantify Rulkov prediction horizons in Lyapunov times, and only two (PRE 108:064304; arXiv:2511.14775) report any attractor-statistic match beyond RMSE.** This is itself a concrete publishable gap in your direction.

## Extending to coupled networks: the two-step roadmap

For the coupled-Rulkov-network phase, the mature recipe is **parallel localized DTS-ESNs à la Pathak et al. 2018 PRL**, with one reservoir per neuron (or per small patch) receiving the neuron's state plus a buffer of its graph neighbours. Srinivasan, Panahi, Ji, Roy et al. have extended this to complex networks. Vlachas, Pathak, Hunt, Sapsis, Girvan, Ott & Koumoutsakos, *Neural Networks* 126:191 (2020) "Backpropagation algorithms and Reservoir Computing in RNNs for forecasting complex spatiotemporal dynamics" benchmark parallel RC against backprop-trained RNNs on Lorenz-96 and KS, confirming parallel RC's scalability advantage.

A different direction is **Graph Echo State Networks** (Gallicchio & Micheli, IJCNN 2010; Ring Reservoir Neural Networks for Graphs, IJCNN 2020; Tortorella & Micheli, ESANN 2021 "Dynamic Graph Echo State Networks"; *Neurocomputing* 2022 "Discrete-time dynamic graph ESNs"). These bake the graph topology into an untrained recursive reservoir, which is ideal when the coupling structure of your Rulkov network is known. For the *reconstruction* case — when the graph is unknown — the 2025 **PRE paper "Utilizing coupled reservoir computing to predict network dynamics and structure"** (DOI 10.1103/6hpq-4cv6) integrates network structure into the ESN readout and uses Granger causality to infer topology from time series simultaneously with prediction. This is effectively an RC analog of your Topal-Eroglu 2023 PRL approach and is the most direct competitor to SINDy-based reconstruction for coupled Rulkov.

Panahi & Lai, *Chaos* 34:051501 (2024), "Adaptable reservoir computing," extend parameter-aware RC to predict bifurcations and tipping points in networked systems — useful if you later want to predict transitions between bursting regimes as coupling varies. Ma et al. (*Chaos, Solitons & Fractals* 2023, "Dynamics reconstruction in the presence of bistability by using reservoir computer") use a parameter-aware RC with a coupling-strength input channel to reconstruct complete bifurcation diagrams of coupled Rössler rings from a single reservoir — directly transferable to coupled Rulkov with the coupling strength as the explicit parameter input. Lampel et al. (*Scientific Reports* via arXiv:2311.03131, "Reservoir-Computing Model for Mapping and Forecasting Neuronal Interactions from Electrophysiological Data") is an experimental-data precedent for RC on neuronal networks. Transfer-learning work is still immature: the only relevant mechanism is **multiple-trajectory training** (Norton et al. 2025 arXiv:2501.16325 "Tailored forecasting from short time series via meta-learning"), in which a single RC is trained across many system instances so it generalizes to unseen regimes — a natural bridge from single-node to coupled-network training.

## Concrete trial order for your problem

**Step 1 (this week): DTS-ESN + LMNT noise regularisation + Recycle Validation.** Implement Tanaka 2022's sampler: a 300–500-unit leaky ESN with α_i ~ LogUniform(10⁻³, 1) on a log scale spanning the full Rulkov fast-slow range, ρ ≈ 0.9, input bias on, input scaling tuned, sparsity 5–10%. Train the linear readout by Tikhonov ridge with added Wikner LMNT regularization. Washout ≥ 3000 steps. Hyperparameters by Bayesian optimization on a Recycle-Validation objective with chaotic-aware windows (Racca & Magri 2021). Target: reproduce return map of inter-burst maxima and ISI distribution; report valid time in units of 1/λ_max and largest Lyapunov exponent of the learned model via the Jacobian method (Margazoglou & Magri 2023).

**Step 2 (if Step 1 horizon < 5 Lyapunov times): add an attractor-level loss.** Extend the objective with a Sinkhorn/Wasserstein penalty on the ISI histogram over a 10³–10⁴-step closed-loop rollout during training (Jiang et al. 2023; Platt et al. 2023 "Constraining chaos"). The ridge closed form is lost; use L-BFGS or SGD over the readout weights.

**Step 3 (optional fallback for horizon): hybrid knowledge-based DTS-ESN.** Pass an approximate Rulkov K[u(t)] — e.g. β=4.0 or a piecewise-polynomial proxy for β/(1+u²) valid on [−2,2] with saturation outside — as an additional input to the readout (Pathak et al. 2018 Chaos). Expect roughly a doubling of valid time, at the cost of black-boxness.

**Step 4 (alternative single-reservoir architecture): two-layer DeepESN.** Bottom layer with α ≈ 0.8, top layer with α ≈ 0.01. Direct comparator to DTS-ESN; useful if DTS-ESN's soft sampling of timescales under-represents the extremes.

**Step 5 (coupled network): parallel DTS-ESN on the known graph + graph-ESN readout.** One reservoir per neuron receiving that neuron's state and a buffer of neighbour states (Pathak 2018 PRL topology adapted to graph edges). For unknown topology use the 2025 PRE coupled-RC + Granger scheme. Parameter-aware input channel for coupling strength (Ma et al. 2023) for bifurcation-diagram reconstruction.

## What to skip, and what is left open

Skip pure NG-RC with polynomial features (Zhang-Cornelius Catch-22). Skip LSMs and spiking reservoirs for prediction. Skip ESGP unless you explicitly need calibrated uncertainty bands beyond ensemble spread. Skip single-α leaky ESN; it is the control that demonstrates the problem, not the solution.

The open frontier, and a natural paper: nobody has reported **Lyapunov-time-normalised closed-loop horizons, return-map reproduction, ISI-distribution Wasserstein distance, and Lyapunov-spectrum matching for the Rulkov map using DTS-ESN with LMNT regularisation and an attractor-matching loss**, and nobody has done it on **coupled Rulkov networks with parallel DTS-ESN**. Both are within reach with the prescription above, and both would sit cleanly between the Tanaka 2022 single-node and Topal-Eroglu 2023 network-reconstruction lines of work.
